DOMAIN: Commitment to Pupils and Pupil Learning

Competency: Teachers demonstrate commitment to the well-being and development of all pupils.

Ms. Wellington demonstrates a strong commitment to the well-being and holistic development of all students. She begins the year by sending questionnaires to families, gathering information about learning styles, interests, and extracurricular activities to better understand each learner. Her practice of greeting students by name with a smile, conducting morning check-ins about feelings, and allowing flexible eating schedules shows her awareness that emotional well-being directly impacts learning. She creates a culturally responsive classroom where texts like "Muslim girls in sports" and "Black Boy Joy" ensure all students see themselves represented.

She differentiates instruction through procedural and product choice, uses gradual release of responsibility, and provides mentor texts to support diverse learners. Ms. Wellington teaches vocabulary explicitly using models like the Freyer model, and maintains positive interactions by praising effort and growth mindset, reminding students that mistakes are part of learning. Her caring approach extends to private conversations in hallways to preserve student dignity, and she encourages reflection on how behavior impacts others.

Competency: Teachers are dedicated in their efforts to teach and support pupil learning and achievement.

Ms. Wellington is dedicated to creating a positive and inclusive learning environment where students feel celebrated and valued. She marks class accomplishments—from hockey wins to gymnastics medals—fostering community pride. Her classroom features a calming corner, noise-cancelling headphones, and fidgets, ensuring students have tools for self-advocacy and emotional regulation.

She builds student capacity through ongoing goal setting and a growth mindset approach to mistakes. Her instruction incorporates experiential learning: nature walks, pumpkin math, clay work, and maker space projects like the human body assignment with moving parts. These hands-on experiences make learning tangible and memorable while developing problem-solving skills.

Competency: Teachers treat all pupils equitably and with respect.

Ms. Wellington maintains positive relationships by celebrating students' unique identities and ensuring all feel respected. She uses non-verbal cues to redirect behavior privately, avoiding public call-outs. Her awareness of diverse needs is evident in accommodations for fasting students, interest in Arabic school, and encouragement of students sharing languages like patois, Urdu, and Tamil.

She provides tiered supports including flexible grouping by level, interest, and personality; seating accommodations; chunking of tasks; and timers for organization. Students experience varied formats—independent, partnered, and group work—with opportunities to share learning through think-pair-share, conversations, and one-on-one discussions. Her approach reflects a deep commitment to equity and inclusion.

Competency: Teachers provide an environment for learning that encourages pupils to be problem-solvers, decision-makers, lifelong-learners, and contributing members of a changing society.

Ms. Wellington encourages students to connect classroom learning to real-world contexts. In Math, students discuss taxes with parents and estimate grocery costs. They evaluated non-renewable energy sources and explored global implications. Weekly Waterloo Problems of the Week develop problem-solving skills, with parent communication emphasizing their importance.

Her maker space projects—like the human body assignment requiring moving parts—challenge students to plan, build, and iterate. Students pursue independent inquiries such as disease research slideshows, developing agency and curiosity that extend beyond curriculum boundaries.

DOMAIN: Professional Knowledge

Competency: Teachers know their subject matter, the Ontario curriculum, and education-related legislation.

Ms. Wellington demonstrates thorough knowledge of the Ontario curriculum and FSL expectations. She embeds scope and sequence in planning, integrates heritage months into Long Range Plans, and uses board-recommended resources: Catch Up Your Code, Know Your Words, Think SSRD, and Reading Coach. Her French literacy toolkit includes Éclair, Son-Au-Graphe, Idéllo, and Alpha Graphe.

Students learn through cross-curricular connections—Math via read-alouds, engineering through hands-on Science tasks—and explicit teaching of learning goals, success criteria, and vocabulary. Her approach reflects current research emphasizing oral and listening skills in French immersion, supported by daily opportunities for authentic communication.

Competency: Teachers know a variety of effective teaching and assessment practices.

Ms. Wellington employs triangulation of assessment—conversations, products, and observations—to comprehensively understand student learning. She uses exit tickets, thumbs up/down check-ins, and flexible grouping. Math talk conversations probe deeper thinking, while on-the-spot feedback guides improvement before final submission.

Assessment is for, of, and as learning: rubrics, checklists, self-assessments, and co-created success criteria. Students have multiple opportunities to demonstrate learning through choice in presentation format—Google Sites, Slides, Canva, print, or oral. S.T.E.A.M. integration appears across subjects: human body projects, renewable energy assignments, Black Boy Joy skits, and Treaty Words art.

Competency: Teachers know a variety of effective classroom management strategies.

Ms. Wellington creates consistent, predictable environments while adjusting supports for individual needs. Posted expectations and schedules provide structure; changes are communicated early. She uses nonverbal redirection—hand motions, proximity, light touch—to maintain flow without disrupting learning.

Movement breaks and brain breaks support self-regulation, while restorative practices address conflicts. Monthly community circles reinforce school values like anti-bullying. She balances individual and group points to build collaborative culture, praising positive behaviors aligned with class values such as kindness and helpfulness.

Competency: Teachers know how pupils learn and the factors that influence pupil learning and achievement.

Ms. Wellington understands that students learn through multiple modalities and opportunities for consolidation. She models making mistakes to normalize growth mindset, emphasizes process over product, and encourages resubmission after feedback. Integration of music (Manie Musicale, Flocabulary, Zoey Roy songwriting), drama (Black Boy Joy retelling), arts (Treaty Words projects), and environmental learning (nature walks) engages diverse learners.

Hands-on tasks, manipulatives, and varied consolidation strategies—video, song, discussion, think-pair-share—ensure concepts stick. Her responsive teaching provides multiple entry points so students connect personally with content.

DOMAIN: Teaching Practice

Competency: Teachers use their professional knowledge and understanding of pupils, curriculum, legislation, teaching practices, and classroom management strategies to promote the learning and achievement of their pupils.

Ms. Wellington applies her knowledge to promote student achievement through focused, purposeful instruction. She teaches metacognition: brainstorming and planning before writing, reflecting during reading, and prompting students to explain their thinking and identify needs. Co-created learning goals and success criteria provide clarity.

Visual and verbal time reminders keep lessons on track. She emphasizes oral/listening skills in FSL, provides vocabulary lists for presentations, and models constructive feedback and respectful disagreement. Her instruction is responsive, providing entry points that connect to students' lives and experiences.

Competency: Teachers communicate effectively with pupils, parents, and colleagues.

Ms. Wellington demonstrates positive, professional communication with all stakeholders. Email correspondence is timely and professional; difficult conversations include concrete examples of support strategies. Expectations are shared via email and visual weekly slides on Google Classroom, with resources provided for parents.

She follows board reporting guidelines diligently, submitting prompt report cards with strengths, growth areas, and next steps in inclusive, non-discriminatory language. Her collaborative approach extends to grade 5 team discussions, ISSP and ELL support staff coordination, and implementation of literacy coach recommendations.

Competency: Teachers conduct ongoing assessment of their pupils' progress, evaluate their achievement, and report results to pupils and parents regularly.

Ms. Wellington uses varied assessment techniques: rubrics, checklists, self-assessments, checkbricks, tests, and anecdotal records. Success criteria are shared proactively and co-created with students. On-the-spot check-ins—thumbs up/down, finger scales, whiteboards—gauge understanding continuously.

Feedback is meaningful and timely: positive acknowledgment followed by next steps. During oral presentations, she balances open-ended questions with tiered support. Students typically receive one additional improvement opportunity before final submission, though she gradually releases this responsibility as students internalize reflection.

Competency: Teachers adapt and refine their teaching practices through continuous learning and reflection, using a variety of sources and resources.

Ms. Wellington modifies programming for exceptional students through "good for one/good for all" approaches, explicit self-advocacy teaching, and accessible technology. She stays current with research: daily oral/listening opportunities in French, weekly Waterloo Problems with explicit problem-solving models, and culturally responsive texts addressing global issues.

Her practice reflects ongoing professional reading and OCT library resources. Collaboration with social workers, literacy coaches, and support staff informs individualized strategies. She participates in school-wide staff development and implements learning directly in classroom practice.

Competency: Teachers use appropriate technology in their teaching practices and related professional responsibilities.

Ms. Wellington integrates technology to improve planning, instruction, and assessment. Online assignments organized in slideshows support student independence; Google Forms, Padlet, Knowledgehook, and Smash Education gather assessment data. SharePoint access connects her to board resources including FSL and Indigenous SharePoints.

Students complete responsible technology training before using devices, with clear consequences for misuse. She promotes French resources (SMASH Education, Boukilili, Lalilo, Idéllo, Word Reference), Math supports (Knowledgehook, Khan Academy, Waterloo Problems), and literacy tools (Reading Coach, Readworks) for classroom and home use.

Ms. Wellington actively collaborates to strengthen school community. Competency: Teachers work with professionals, parents, and members of the community to enhance pupil learning, pupil achievement, and school programs.
