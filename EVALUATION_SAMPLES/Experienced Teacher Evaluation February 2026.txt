Mr. Hackett’s Wall of Awesome

The most popular part of my class. Students work hard to show ways of being “awesome” (i.e. being kind, helping others, answers questions, winning games, etc). If I catch them doing something awesome, they choose a number between 1-100. On Fridays I spin a wheel from 1-100 and play Thunderstruck by ACDC. 3 students win each week. When the 100 spots are filled we have a mega draw and 11 students win. It is a great motivator and reward system. Attendance on Fridays tend to be very high.

Esti-Mystery Math

Students get 5 written clues and a paper with numbers 1-50. They have to estimate the number in the vase. After each clue, they have to change their estimate if necessary. At the end, students have 2 numbers and discuss with each other, which number makes the most sense and why. This uses great math vocabulary for the students and gets them thinking.

Technology is used to differentiate instruction, provide visual and auditory scaffolds, and give students opportunities to demonstrate understanding in multiple ways, ensuring equitable access for all learners.
I also use technology to facilitate communication with parents, caregivers, and colleagues, including email updates, digital portfolios, online conferencing, and learning management systems, providing timely feedback and sharing student progress. Resources and materials are selected through an equity lens, ensuring that digital content is culturally responsive and represents diverse perspectives, including those of Black students and other historically marginalized groups.
Through the thoughtful integration of technology, I support student engagement, critical thinking, and collaboration while maintaining professional responsibilities such as assessment, reporting, and communication. Technology is leveraged as a tool to enhance inclusivity, representation, and meaningful learning experiences for every student in my Grade 1 classroom.

Being a Positive Role Model
Many students last year were telling each other that Henna is for girls, so many boys were not getting it done. So I stopped one day in the hallway where they were doing it, and I said give me a sleeve of henna. That way students would see a male teacher getting henna and might think that they should do it too, no matter their gender.
